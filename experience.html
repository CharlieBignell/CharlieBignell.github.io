Imperial College London - Health Intelligence Analyst

September 2020 - present

Since September 2020, I have worked as part of an MRC-funded project to produce public-facing data visualisation tools using neonatal data. The overarching aim of this project is to increase the utility of the National Neonatal Research Database (NNRD), which contains patient data for infants admitted to a neonatal ward - comprising nearly 1.5 million entries, with over 450 variables per entry.

Working with this large amount of data has been an extremely rewarding yet challenging task. I have greatly improved upon my programming and data analysis skills, including Python, R, and SQL. I have also spent a lot of time gathering requirements and feedback from a wide range of people. This includes parents of premature children, data managers, clinicians and researchers. I have also worked with personnel from 15 different countries, to produce a similar suite of tools using worldwide neonatal data. This experience has helped me to grow in confidence, improving my communication and presentation skills. It has also been an interesting challenge to work with people from so many different backgrounds - in terms of both professional discipline and culture.

Some of the tools I have produced have been published for public use. You can view them here.

As I near the end of my contract in this role, I have also been working on a new research publication that I'm extremely proud of. I have been investigating the growth pattern of preterm infants, and comparing these results to the existing growth charts used in neonatal practice today.

Overall, this has been a rewarding role where I have learnt a lot of new skills. I feel I have transitioned from fresh graduate to fully-fledged computer scientist, and I'm excited to take on my next challenge.

Science and Technology Facilities Council (STFC) - Software Developer

June 2018 - June 2019

Part of my four-year degree included a year-long placement in industry. I spent my time at STFC, where I worked as part of a three-person team called Performance Improvement, working on the ISIS Neutron and Muon Source. Our role was to find areas within the organisation that could be streamlined with a bespoke project. My time was spent updating and maintaining various tools that were used across the site.

The majority of my time was spent independently working on a web application that provided statistical analyses of alarm data. These ~2000 alarms are connected to sensors across the particle accelerator, but engineers had to use very old software to search through these alarms and find the issue. The web application I built helped to streamline this process, and since I left it has been deployed across the site for use by hundreds of scientists and engineers.

I regularly discussed details of my projects with both technical and non-technical personnel, and spent time gathering requirements and use-cases from them. I believe this experience of 'traditional' software development has been complemented by my more data-focussed role at Imperial College London to broaden my skill set.
